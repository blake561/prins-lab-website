Prins Lab

Investigating the mechanisms of right ventricular failure and pulmonary hypertension through integrative multi-omic, preclinical, and translational approaches.

The Prins Lab, led by Kurt Prins MD PhD, is dedicated to understanding how pulmonary arterial hypertension drives right ventricular failure and multi-organ dysfunction. We combine molecular biology, metabolomics, and large-animal models to identify new therapeutic targets.

Overview

Pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH) is increasingly recognized as a systemic disease. Our lab investigates how PAH-driven right ventricular failure leads to dysfunction in distant organs — particularly the liver. We have shown that PAH induces a metabolic and inflammatory hepatopathy characterized by disrupted branched-chain amino acid metabolism and ferroptosis, and we are working to define targetable pathways that connect cardiopulmonary hemodynamics to end-organ damage.

Overview

Ferroptosis — a form of iron-dependent cell death driven by lipid peroxidation — has emerged as a central mechanism linking metabolic derangements and pathological inflammation in pulmonary hypertension. Our lab discovered that ferroptosis promotes PH development and demonstrated that ferroptosis inhibition can rescue RV function in large-animal models, opening new therapeutic avenues.

Overview

Pathological microtubule remodeling disrupts the mechanical and signaling architecture of cardiomyocytes in the failing right ventricle. Our lab has shown that microtubule depolymerization with colchicine rescues RV function, and that the microtubule–junctophilin-2 axis links T-tubule disruptions to mitochondrial dysfunction. We have also identified GP130 inflammatory signaling as a driver of this process and demonstrated that GP130 antagonism enhances RV function in large-animal models.

Overview

Emerging evidence points to gut dysbiosis as a contributor to pulmonary vascular disease and right ventricular dysfunction. Our lab was among the first to characterize the proinflammatory gut microbiome and altered circulating microbial metabolites in PAH patients. We have shown that Lactobacillus restructures the micro- and mycobiome to combat inflammation-mediated RV dysfunction, and demonstrated that microbiota transplant therapy is safe and feasible in PAH.

Overview

The failing right ventricle undergoes profound metabolic reprogramming. Our lab uses multi-omic approaches — proteomics, metabolomics, and transcriptomics — to define the metabolic landscape of RV dysfunction across species. We have shown that intermittent fasting and AMPK activation can restructure RV lipid metabolism and microtubules, and identified novel targets like WNK1 and O-GlcNAcylation that drive metabolic derangements in PAH.
